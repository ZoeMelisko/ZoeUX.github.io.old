The Challenge

My main challenge during this project was implementing modern e-commerce standards that improve overall usability while maintaining Burger Records unique brand identity.

My Process

Initially my process was a straight forward UX format rooted in user research, analysis, and design. However, as the project unfolded my process became more adaptive to unexpected challenges and I embraced an iterative approach to nearly all phases of the project.

Research

I began my research process by exploring the Burger Records site at a high level to understand its basic purpose, function(s), and flow before diving into research methodologies. I was surprised that even at such a broad level I was confused and even lost on an external site within a few clicks.

Where are the problem areas?

To focus the problems I faced during the initial site walk through, I decided to conduct a Heuristic Evaluation. This helped me address friction points that are strictly related to usability and break them down into solvable parts rather than tackling all the problems at once.

Key takeaway:

Most usability issues found stemmed from changing navigation and layout patterns across the main Burger Records site and their shop.

How is the site structured?

I put together a site map to illustrate the information architecture and hierarchy of pages. It wasn’t until this breakdown that I realized that the “Shop” and “Shirts/Totes” navigation links redirect to two separate e-commerce sites hosted by 3rd party merchandise distributors.

These 2 shops have user interfaces that are deceptively similar to Burger Records main site and the only indication that you left the main site is a slight change in the URL. This stood out to me as a huge red flag when it comes to a users sense of trust and security when making purchases through these sites.

Who is the competition?

I conducted 2 different competitive and comparative analysis, one to tackle standard features used across the independent music distribution space and another focused on layout comparison to begin strategizing Burger Records new content hierarchy.

Key Takeaway:

While Burger Records contains nearly all the features found on their competitors sites, the features are often broken or inefficient.

How do users navigate currently?

I created a User Flow chart to evaluate what avenues users take to purchase products in the apparel shop versus the music shop and to consider what path they might take to purchase from both sites.

The two shops shared similarly dysfunctional e-commerce features which caused a laborious process for users to find a product they actually wanted and could purchase. Additionally, if a user wanted to purchase items from both shops they would have to complete two different account creation and transaction processes.

Feature Prioritization?

I put together a feature prioritization axis that scaled features based on how essential they are to a users experience versus how much effort and expense it would cost to accomplish.

This process helped me further narrow down e-commerce features that provides the user with more intuitive navigation and simplify the purchasing process.

Persona

Utilizing affinity mapping I was able to find patterns between the various people I interviewed which allowed me to pinpoint Burger Records target users and form a reliable user persona to base design solutions on.

Journey Mapping

In a journey map I was able to further empathize with our target users when they use the current Burger Records site.

Designing Solutions

Ideation and Paper Prototyping

I proceeded to ideate navigation schemas and content layouts that would best serve users. I A/B tested different content hierarchy designs on the homepage and explored different types of checkout flows.

Overall, users navigated easily through product listing screens, however, they wanted a more robust product detail screen and needed adjustments to the checkout flow. This process was incredible helpful to find points of improvements with functionality and flow.

Final Designs

High Fidelity

The results from my usability tests required me to pivot my design process. In my second round of high fidelity designing, I kept user interviews and the persona more inline with the design thinking and was strict when it came to maintaining the unique feel of the label.

I focused on curating a more authentic record shop experience online by creating robust product pages that highlighted artists and their craft to mimic conversations that often happen between record consumers and shop employees. Further testing of this design was met with much better reactions from fans and new users alike along with a more seamless experience overall.

Prototype

  • A streamlined navigation and content hierarchy that promotes user discovery.

  • Robust product detail pages allowing users to explore more about artists creating intimate music experience

  • A full guest checkout flow to help assure users order completion

Revised Site Map

I redesigned the site’s navigation to provide more robust navigation options to accommodate various types of users. This navigation not only helps users like Sean to easily find and purchase products but it also benefits users who are looking to discover events, news, or find out how to join the label.

New User Flow

The new user experience is much more focused on an e-commerce purpose while still maintaining authenticity to the label and their artists. Users immediately knew where to go to find products and can quickly find specific items or just explore new artists like browsing a record shop. The site also encourages users to discover more by featuring products, artists, and also providing recommendations.
