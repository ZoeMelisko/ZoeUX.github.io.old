Brief

As a solo designer, I restructured and designed a fresh look for this independent music labels e-commerce site.

View Prototype

Overview

The Challenge

During this project I tackled implementing modern e-commerce standards while staying authentic to Burger Records' unique brand identity that their existing users love.



The Solution

In a 2-week sprint, I overhauled the site from landing page to order confirmation to help users better navigate, discover, and purchase music from the unique indie label.

My Process

Research

Although improving e-commerce functionality was the main focus of this project, I didn't want to assume that it was my only focus. I performed an audit of the site to understand all the content it provides its users; I found there was little that the site didn't offer which created a confusing experience even at a high level. See how I documented more of my research findings below!

Usability Test

Where are the problem areas?

Through a comprehensive heuristic evaluation, I was able to identify distinct friction points and break them down into solvable categories that helped me prioritize what areas of opportunity would have the most impact on users.

Key takeaway:

Most usability issues stemmed from changing navigation and layout patterns, therefore, creating consistency for users became my #1 priority.

How is the current site structured?

By diagramming the sites architecture, I found the “Shop” and “Shirts/Totes” categories in the navigation redirected to two separate e-commerce sites hosted by two different merchandise distributors. Both 3rd party sites had interfaces that were similar to the actual Burger Records' website but varied in layout and navigation.

I considered the use of deceptive 3rd party sites a huge red flag due to the lack of consistency, security, and transparency Burger Records was providing its users.

Who is the competition?

I conducted two competitive and comparative analysis, one focused on features and the other comparing layouts that are commonly used across the music distribution e-commerce space.

Key Takeaway:

Most features that competitors had were present but were unreliable when used. Additionally, the current layout of the landing page and product pages lacked the consistency that other music label sites had.

How do users navigate currently?

In usability tests, users found navigating the main Burger Records site and the two e-commerce shops confusing. Once users realized they were required to create accounts on two 3rd party sites they didn't recognize to complete two checkout processes they gave up entirely. In one user's words "...this whole process seems shady to me...finding items isn't easy, and I don't know who got my [purchasing] info. I just don't trust it."

Feature Prioritization?

I put together a feature prioritization axis that scaled features based on how essential they were to a users experience versus how much effort and expense it would cost to accomplish.

This process helped me further narrow down e-commerce features that provides the user with more intuitive navigation and simplify the product discovery process.

Persona

Utilizing affinity mapping I was able to find patterns between the various users I interviewed which allowed me to pinpoint Burger Records target users and form a reliable user persona to base design solutions on.

Journey Mapping

In a journey map I was able to further diagram Burger Records target users thoughts and emotions as they interacted with the current Burger Records site.

Designing Solutions

Ideation and Paper Prototyping

I proceeded to ideate navigation schemas and content layouts that would best serve users. I A/B tested a variety of content hierarchies of the homepage and explored different types of checkout flows.

After various rounds of low fidelity usability testing, the user flow became smoother and I was able to quickly iterate on feedback before commiting to my initail design sketches.

Final Designs



High Fidelity

The results from my usability tests required me to pivot my design process. In my second round of high fidelity designing, I kept user interviews and the persona more inline with the design thinking and was strict when it came to maintaining the unique feel of the label.

I focused on curating a more authentic record shop experience online by creating robust product pages that highlighted artists and their craft to mimic conversations that often happen between record consumers and shop employees. Further testing of this design was met with much better reactions from fans and new users alike along with a more seamless experience overall.

The Results

Prototype

I presented the redesign including my research findings, testing results, and final high fidelity prototype.

  • A streamlined navigation and content hierarchy that promotes user discovery.

  • Product listing pages with various filter options to help users find exactly what they want quickly.

  • Robust product detail pages allowing users to explore more about artists creating intimate music experience

  • A full guest checkout flow to help assure users order completion

Revised Site Map

I redesigned the site’s navigation to provide more robust navigation options to accommodate various types of users.

This not only helped users easily find and purchase products but it also clarified where users could discover events, latest news, or find out how to join the label.

New User Flow

The new user experience is much more centered around e-commerce and encouraging user discovery by highlighting their bands and musicians.

During usability tests users who were and weren't familiar with Burger Records immediately knew where to go to find products and quickly found specific items they were looking for. The new design also led users to discover more by featuring products and artists on the homepage and recommending items on product pages.

Reflection

As my first UX project, I found that I enjoyed researching and understanding users. However, I underestimated the importance of speaking with users from Burger Records' key demographic. Most users I interviewed were analog "hobbyists" that were unfamiliar with the label's personality which caused a lot of negative feedback on their branding and purposely dated design. My initial interface designs toned down the company's branding which ended up taking all the fun out of the site. My second iteration was much more successful and true to the company's aesthetics and fanbase.


Next Steps

I really enjoyed working on a site that has such a unique presence in its marketplace. In another design sprint I would:

  • Continue testing with users that are more inline with the analog music consumer demographic and tackle more of the business end functionality, such as the demo submission process.

  • Explore the implementation of more personalized elements of the site such as band recommendations based on a users purchases.

  • Further hone in on the branding and design of the site and provide fun interaction design to bring more life into the experience.
